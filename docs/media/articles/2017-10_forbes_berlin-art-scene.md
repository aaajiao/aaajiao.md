# Berlin's Thriving Contemporary Art Scene is a Land of the Free, Home of the Brave

**来源**: Forbes
**日期**: October 18, 2017
**作者**: Declane Ytan
**原文链接**: https://www.forbes.com/sites/declaneytan/2017/10/18/berlins-thriving-contemporary-art-scene-is-a-land-of-the-free-home-of-the-brave/

---

![Burning the US Constitution by Dread Scott](https://blogs-images.forbes.com/declaneytan/files/2017/10/Burning-the-US-Constitution.png-1200x800.jpg)
*Burning the US Constitution. Dread Scott*

"I'm still in the process of learning what Berlin means to me as an artist. But it's important for me to get out there - out of America. I want to be part of a broader conversation. I think it's important for radical black artists to have a voice in Europe. A voice that is not mediated by the news, but comes directly from the source," says Brooklyn-based artist Dread Scott when we meet at the Black Matters exhibtion, on display at Berlin's Galerie Barbara Thumm.

The exhibition, realized in cooperation with curator Octavio Zaya, is centered around the topic of "the black experience", seen through the eyes of a collective of artists of African descent. Burning the US Constitution - a work by Dread Scott included in the exhibition - references Ai Wei Wei's 1995 Dropping a Han Dynasty Urn. Similar to the work by the Chinese artist slash activist, Scott's piece is centered around a series of photographs which symbolize the necessity of destroying the old in order to create something new.

The Galerie Barbara Thumm which represents Dread Scott in Germany, was amongst the 112 galleries present in the form of a booth at the recently concluded Art Berlin fair. The fair's inaugural edition, founded by former Art Basel Communication Director Maike Cruse, marks the beginning of a new chapter for the contemporary art capital, increasingly popular amongst young expats in search of artistic freedom.

"Berlin is not a traditional market like Switzerland, London, or New York. The cost of living, the space available for artists and the overall openness of the city creates a perfect environment for artistic experimentation," explains Cruse.

![Art Berlin's inaugural edition](https://blogs-images.forbes.com/declaneytan/files/2017/10/Art-Berlin-1200x800.png)
*Art Berlin's inaugural edition. Art Berlin.*

Chinese artist Xu Wenkai is part of a more recent wave of international artists making their move to the German art hub. Wenkai is described as a media artist, blogger and activist by the Berlin-based Edmond Gallery which represents the artist locally. His work - typically centered around the themes of internet, new thinking and China's Great Firewall - is motivated by an anti-technology stance, and was amongst those on display at the Berlin art fair.

"Right now for me, Berlin is very important, because it allows me to experiment and try things that I've never tried before. And I can afford it, in terms of rent and cost of living," says Wenkai, better known by his pseudonym aaajiao.

![aaajiao's 404 installation](https://blogs-images.forbes.com/declaneytan/files/2017/10/Xu-Wenkai-.jpg)
*aaajiao's 404 installation. Edmond Gallery*

Art Berlin founder Maike Cruse couldn't agree more with aaajiao's Berlin-related comment, concluding, "There aren't too many rules here: there are many illegal bars and illegal clubs with no closing hour. Plus there are many empty spaces available for artists. You don't see that in London or Paris."
