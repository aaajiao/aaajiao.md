# aaajiao: Intelligence, Power and Reality

### 1. How individuals adapt in the AI era: tools, methods, mindset

#### Information Literacy 3.0

Yang

There’s a term—information literacy. At the most basic level it means, for example: Do I know how to use ChatGPT? One layer deeper, it’s about how a person gets information on the internet. How do we build this literacy? Do we acquire it naturally, or can it be taught?

aaajiao

I remember, quite early on—perhaps somewhere between Internet 1.0 and 2.0, maybe earlier, though I can’t fix the date—people used the term “functional illiteracy,” meaning some people show weak ability when using tools. I think that still applies. Today the gaps in how people obtain information are enormous. With large models, this ability is tested even more. The reason is simple: with the same AI, different prompts yield completely different results—even where you enter the prompt matters, which many people don’t realise.

Another point: with even the simplest conversational model, the kind of question you ask is crucial. Interaction has become simpler. In the past, when using a tool, you had to learn its details and undergo training to unlock more capability. Now the tool has been compressed into the most familiar interface of all: conversation. But conversation is hard. If you don’t understand the mechanism behind the system, you won’t ask the right question.

Let me give a concrete example. On the traditional internet, you open a browser, use a search engine, type keywords, and get a flood of results. Your query is a question of sorts, but what you receive are discrete answers with weak connections. You have to build the links yourself to reach what you want. In some ways it’s like using a dictionary: you rely on the index to find many entries and, as you read more, you may refine your question. That process is a kind of learning; it helps you clarify what you truly want.

Of course, it’s imprecise. If your ability to handle information is weak, you won’t get good results.

Today, with a conversational model, you have to ask the right question—and even know how to split a big problem into parts to get better results, because there are token limits. Using it is still a kind of training. But because it feels like everyday talk, many people skip the training. The chance to learn how to use the tool gets weaker.

When the question is poor and the result is poor, trust erodes. People stop seeing what a large model is good for. Who best understands how to ask? People who code.

I’ve followed these people on social media for a long time. There are many AI-assisted coding tools now, and programmers share genuinely practical feedback: how to use them, how to slice problems, how to scope them, how to classify sub-tasks. It looks like an engineering discipline of usage. For most general users, that’s hard to emulate.

Yang

Exactly. I follow a lot of developers on X (formerly Twitter) and I’ve learned a lot—structured prompting, and then “context engineering.” I used to toss in a PDF or a video and just ask for a summary. Later I tried one of their prompts and the outcome stunned me: highly detailed, well structured, even surfacing the underlying mindset.

No one really teaches this; it felt like an accidental discovery. The bigger issue is that people often lack the awareness to try—our schooling doesn’t instil it. What should we do?

aaajiao

Asking a good question sounds simple, but doing it right requires many preconditions. By watching experts in a field use a tool on social media, you pick things up. It’s like real life: if you want to achieve something, asking the right question is a core competence. It must be learned—ideally taught by people who truly grasp how to do it.

So when we face a relatively new form of human–machine interaction, there’s a trap. As thinking animals, we can be vain. The more familiar the scene feels, the more we assume mastery. We lack curiosity about its structure and instead sink into the comfort of familiar interaction. That’s a kind of mental laziness. Is this hard to learn? Compared with writing a program in a compiled language like C++, it’s much easier. That’s one reason ChatGPT has so many users: the learning curve is low. But recognising that it still requires learning—that is the point we have to keep discussing.

#### Vibe Coding

Yang

You mentioned that the people who really get the most out of AI are still programmers. Over the past couple of years there’s been a buzzword—zhuanma (switching career into coding), especially among humanities graduates. Everyone’s analysing full and partial career switches in the AI era. I want to switch too, because if you want to make art with  technology without understanding any of it, you’re very constrained.

I’ve enrolled in an introductory computer-science course, though I haven’t finished it. I also realise not everything can be solved by algorithms—will, love, and freedom resist formalisation. Yet that seems to be shifting: many programmers—especially those straddling science and art—are trying to translate soft, pliable things into code. You may be doing this yourself. So back to my question: how do you think about code? Do you still recommend zhuanma? If an artist wants to learn to code, what would you advise?

aaajiao

From my practice, large AI models—especially for coding—have been a major release of productive power. At the same time, from my “second-hand” experience on social media, there’s a big debate right now on Simplified-Chinese X (formerly Twitter): highly specialised programmers feel their work has become less replaceable. They also acknowledge that for entry-level coders, displacement by large models is real. I’ve seen layoff figures from big U.S. tech firms suggesting that perhaps over 60% of those affected were junior programmers, because AI-driven productivity replaced them quickly. Senior, experienced people are much harder to replace. So, to some extent, it depends on how far you want to go.

From my point of view, I wouldn’t use the word zhuanma. It’s a pre-LLM story tied to jobs, mobility, and survival. Today there are more chances to try things you wouldn’t have attempted before. Take vibe coding: just try it. Understand as much as you can. As a tool, it’s pedagogical—it opens domains that used to feel out of reach. So don’t just circle around it, use it. Whether you go deep depends entirely on your career plan and your curiosity. But it does bring a real kind of freedom—especially in programming.

#### How to Learn Deeply in an Age of Information Overload

Yang

How do you think today’s ways of learning differ from before? You also have to absorb a lot of new fields at high intensity. How do you keep that up—do you have a method, or do you just follow your interests and read as you go?

aaajiao

I have very specific habits. I care about so-called “new” things, and how they are built and used by people. Social media is still a great place to pick up this kind of information—but only if you use it deeply, choose the right platforms, and filter for value. Even now, if you want the very latest on AI, most of it still surfaces on X (formerly Twitter). 

Meta’s Threads, in Chinese circles, functions a bit like early Douban—tech discussions are rare there, whereas X has a lot—so building a steady habit of close observation and reading on social platforms really matters. Another point that’s crucial to me is: what do you actually care about? The internet is overflowing with information; attention is fragmented. That’s arguably the defining problem of the past decade.

How do you turn what you read into part of yourself—and find a learning method that truly fits you? That’s hard. So hard that even picking the right tool can be difficult. My way is simple: subtraction. Using more tools doesn’t make you smarter, knowing your need right now does. Take my own set-up with “newish” tools. On X, I categorize people in different lists, including one for AI. I track what these people I care about are doing and the larger shifts emerge.

AI has spawned new tools, I now use Perplexity a lot. It’s an answer engine wrapped around search, but the results are strong. I use it for quick research and comparisons, it’s good at pulling sources together via large models. 

Another tool I use heavily is Google’s NotebookLM. It can generate podcast-like audio overviews from your sources. For papers or books I won’t close-read, I have it make an “audio overview.” I found a well-crafted prompt on X, so the outputs are usually 20-plus minutes and can dig in. Recently it added video overviews, and the audio feature has improved. With the same prompt, many books now yield audio overviews close to an hour—dense with information and easy to consume in fragments. 

Those are my mainstays. I do try many small tools. Once I understand their structure, I often drop them. For me, these are transitional tools in AI’s evolution—I don’t need to master each one to improve how I gather information. Mostly, I subtract.

#### Large Models Centralise Power

Yang

You mentioned you found a great NotebookLM prompt. I’ve had similar moments—stumble on a good prompt, and suddenly both density and quality of information soar. But that also makes things feel more uncertain: getting good information seems both easy and hard. Many people might never find that prompt. Is there a way to find it easily?

aaajiao

It’s hard—because it really tests a person’s overall capacity to find, apply, and go deeper with information. And that whole pipeline is, to me, exclusionary. It’s tied to your education, class, and how you use the internet. Where do prompts come from? From large models. Are large models levellers? Since the “ChatGPT moment,” I haven’t thought so.

Large models are born of centralisation—they concentrate compute, meaning money, electricity, energy; chips are made in highly concentrated supply chains. If the system itself is centralised, then prompts—the touchpoint with users—will also concentrate. That makes this stage deeply unfair.

Will fairness improve as the “big model” paradigm fragments into smaller, vertical, domain-specific models? Possibly. But right now, it’s very unequal. If you understand the system, you can work efficiently and research deeply. If you don’t, it’s just a chat bot. Many everyday users treat ChatGPT as a kind of therapist.

#### The ChatGPT “Handbook” Hides Industry Clues

Yang

Let me step back to basics. I told a product manager I want to study “CS,” and he laughed, because that word feels dated. Maybe they use other terms, like, “IT” or “the internet”. Are they the same thing?

aaajiao

In Chinese big-tech talk, people throw around “alignment” and other insider terms. I don’t dwell on it. I majored in CS—computer science—so “CS” seems fine to me.

Jargon keeps multiplying—Silicon Valley has its own dialect. In my practice, I try to use less of what only a subset understands. Instead I pull key concepts out and work with them. In last year’s solo show A bit, A prompt, many titles came from the “ChatGPT Handbook,” which tries to mark what’s important in this industry. The terms need broad legibility: “prompt” is widely understood; “agent” needs an agreed-upon definition. There’s also conflict among these terms. 

When something “new” appears—say, in AI—I still look for its metadata. “Prompt,” for instance, is AI metadata woven through this whole LLM wave. People also talk about AI “agents”—but what exactly is an agent and what does it imply? I look for precise, shared definitions. That’s my entry point.

Yang

“Agent” still lacks consensus. Your work builds on it—aren’t you worried some people won’t get the background and thus miss the piece? Many people don’t know “metadata” either.

aaajiao

We were using "metadata" as early as 2016. Even earlier, in my 2014 work The Screen Generation (屏幕一代), I employed the term "meta" when Facebook was still called Facebook. At the time, I had a basic grasp of its meaning, because even the simplest HTML language had a tag called <meta description>. Its function is that you must fill it out when, for example, doing SEO (Search Engine Optimization). You can also understand it as a “tag”. These concepts, therefore, gain recognition incrementally. This includes Facebook's renaming itself Meta, driven by the so-called Metaverse, which essentially inherited these technical definitions—concepts that had been clearly defined within a relatively limited professional domain for a long time. It is a continuous process of generalization that the public gradually comes to understand through usage and the development of the internet.

#### Compute Power Is the Power to Make Worlds

Another example: computing power. In Shanghai I exhibited Cave Simulator (2020), half a year later, Deep Simulator (2020) followed. We were already talking about computing power back then—before the ChatGPT moment (2020). In those days, the public was fuzzy on what “compute” meant. Today most people have at least a rough sense, even if it’s still hazy for some. New tech terms aren’t “jargon” so much as abstractions for new phenomena. They become understood through repeated public contact—and misreadings along the way. 

Artists misread too—and that’s fine. You can expand concepts. In computer science, the meaning of “compute” is precise. But when I began working with it in 2020, as an artist I was imagining: how do we understand climate? The shows didn’t state it directly, but in interviews I would ask: if the world is constituted by “compute,” is climate an expression of it? Why can’t we control climate yet? Because our compute isn’t sufficient to fully model—let alone control—it. This is a way of thinking that stretches the original concept. In public conversation, ideas mutate, the process itself affects people. This conceptual expansion is part of what makes making artwork interesting.

Yang

Let me check my understanding. Compute is the capacity to compute, backed by energy. The link to climate, as I hear you, is: if climate can be formalised as algorithms and simulated, then with enough compute you could run those complex models. Is that it?

aaajiao

You can treat “compute” more abstractly—metadata-like. Strip away its ties to energy or GPUs, which is its origin. But once the concept exists, can we enlarge it? Is “compute” also an ability to grasp certain rules or even a chaotic system? If I can understand and re-make it, am I not exhibiting a very high degree of compute? If that compute is replicable, could we control climate? Yes, many conceptual jumps there—but that’s why it’s a fruitful artistic thread. The point is not to leave “compute” trapped by its birthplace. It’s to use it as a way of thinking.

Yang

So your “compute” already folds in the ability to deconstruct algorithms—in other words, understanding.

aaajiao

Right.

Yang

How do you understand “algorithm”?

aaajiao

I was never going to be an algorithms engineer. Part of why I moved the internet—its content and culture—into contemporary art is that, when I hit graphics algorithms, it became obvious I wasn’t built for that. Discrete maths at that level, and transferring it into production, was beyond me.

But I realised I’m good at decomposing concepts and linking them to human concerns—using technology to open up possibilities in reality. Within those possibilities, you can talk about social change, human change, and the evolution of the tech itself.

So on “algorithm,” I probably understand it much as you do, but when I use the concept, I use it as a filter to consider society.

Same with “compute”—I might ask whether compute relates to climate, or whether the world itself can be seen as an abstract composition of compute. Through that lens, new facts come into view—facts that already exist in human society but are easy to miss when you discuss “everything” all at once. Concepts act like filters. Each filter lets certain issues pass through—each can become a artwork, a method of thinking, or simply a precise question.

### 2. The Power Logic Behind Algorithms

#### Silicon Valley’s Political Turn

Yang

Two years ago I already saw reporting on how algorithms squeeze food-delivery riders and taxi drivers—that’s one level of it. Then there’s the centralization you mentioned: as algorithms grow more powerful, it feels like the power of programmers and of large models is concentrating. What’s your take?

aaajiao

As algorithmic power expands and compute becomes tightly centralized, I’d frame the cause a bit differently—or rather, here’s how I understand what drives it. It’s a shift in real-world politics: for instance, Silicon Valley’s broader rightward turn and support for Trump. Why would they back Trump? If you think from the vantage point of their business model and production methods, they want to strip back regulation to enable “disruptive innovation.” They’ve coined a new label for this, calling it “anti-progressive progressivism.” So we’re looking at concrete political and social forces at work.

That’s why algorithmic and compute power end up so centralized today: in part the industry has shaped it that way, and in part it’s a mutual embrace with real-world politics. This is also why my work on technology so often intersects with politics: in the end, what we’re looking at is not just the product of technological advance. Tech progress comes with a host of preconditions, and the shock it delivers to social structures is bigger than before—indeed, because people are involved, it remains a human project. Once people in tech recognize the internet is no longer an “emerging industry” but a legacy one, inseparable from the real economy, then influencing politics becomes the natural next step. Influence politics, and you reshape the social order. The ties get tighter, which oddly makes the system more legible: you can analyze it through more than one purely technical lens.

Yang

Bitcoin came to mind. I’ve read that Satoshi opposed money printing and created Bitcoin to resist capitalist exploitation—that sounds utopian. But there’s another line of critique: that firms like Meta pursue pure commercial interest, using algorithms to extract value. Are these two opposing camps? As an outsider, I find it hard to read the ideologies and power relations behind these companies.

aaajiao

Google’s most famous slogan used to be “Don’t be evil.” It’s disarmingly simple—and powerful. Today, for a company of Google’s size, can we still judge it by that yardstick? It’s hard to weigh the world’s fourth-largest by market value using a single moral yardstick. I don’t believe Google sets out to do evil. But whether that one sentence can still assess a firm of this scale is doubtful. Critiquing Big Tech requires more depth—ideally, daily work—because we really do live in a new era of centralization.

On Bitcoin: what’s more interesting, to me, is Wall Street these past two years. The current Trump administration fully embraces crypto. Beyond endorsing “crypto” as a concept, it’s courting the new crypto-rich—and using state power to extend crypto’s remit. Take U.S. “stablecoins”: Wall Street has largely absorbed them. Recently, a company called Circle, which issues USDC, listed in the U.S. equity market. You can see a deep fusion under way between the physical, old-economy world and the value systems of the virtual internet—beliefs people once held as separate now intertwine.

As this fusion deepens, my once naïve picture of tech firms—and the firms’ own cultures—has grown more complex, more concrete. Back to your courier example: that’s a particularly China-specific case. In Germany, many riders have unions. Their rights align with other workers and they can strike. If they’re mistreated, companies pay a price.

But under China’s mode of governance, food-delivery platforms soak up a huge share of working-age labor. That serves a core goal of social stability. And how do you keep people so busy they don’t fuel instability? In that sense, squeezing workers can become a “practical” demand. If you look for the algorithm’s internal driver, it’s not just profit, it’s tangled up with political realities.

#### Open Source

Yang

Agreed—the complexity comes from explaining the same facts from different angles. I’ve also read “surveillance capitalism” pieces that start with AI’s hidden costs: annotation workers drawing boxes, and the heavy metals, energy, and water behind the models. There’s the example of telling Amazon’s voice assistant “Alexa” to turn on a light—the energy it uses far exceeds walking over to flip a switch. I’ve seen a lot of critical essays.

But I also noticed that the DeepMind in London, later acquired by Google. They cracked a major biology problem and open-sourced the results. It used to take a PhD years to solve a tiny piece of that. This feels like the kind of innovation and freedom humanities folks romanticize.

Once I began to learn computer science, I found many important courses are free, and people eager to share—unlike many fields. How do you see “open source” and that ethos?

aaajiao

Open source as a sharing ethos is obviously bound up with the internet: complex projects can be broken into smaller units and more people can join. With the internet, collaboration becomes a clear need, so “open source” becomes an efficient way to collaborate. “Closed source” can be commercially valuable too. Open doesn’t mean “no business value.”

Today I wouldn’t treat it as a singular “internet spirit.” Open and closed are both common tracks. What matters is that your concern about it actually confirms my point about “generalization”: the concept has spread beyond software into social practice. Say you want to found a civic group—how do you structure it? Many people now publish “open-source” playbooks for building social organizations. You can clone their methods under an open-source ethos. That meaningfully reshapes the real world.

Sometimes, though, it’s also a word game. Because “open source” has generalized, we use it to describe things for which we might otherwise invent new terms. Before the “open source” label, did open-style civic organizations exist? Of course. Maybe they simply published all their documents and invited replication. If it’s closed, we’d call it a franchise—think KFC or McDonald’s. If they opened every detail, you could call it an “open-source” business model.

Silicon Valley’s methods, or “tech methods,” do coin lots of terms. These terms work for them—and once generalized, they wield clout. People then reuse them to reframe existing practices. I try not to overstate it. I care about how practices really connect realities, if there’s a sharp conflict or genuine new possibility, let’s talk. If it’s merely a narrative swap, we can set it aside.

#### What Algorithms Can’t See: The Individual

Yang

I’m asking from my own journey: when I suddenly dove into computer science, what flooded in first were new terms and narratives. I warmed to notions like “internet spirit” and “programmer thinking”. Maybe, from your angle, there’s some myth-making there. Which words capture “internet spirit”? Which describe “programmer thinking”?

aaajiao

Hard to say—partly because I’m not of that tribe. And if you ask me to describe the current moment, in plain Mainland Chinese: it’s out of touch with the ground(不接地气). That’s an outsider’s view, yes. If you asked me how artists see technology, I could list some terms. But to speak as a technologist—I’m an outsider, and I’d still say it’s out of touch. The reason, to me, is that the revolution in productivity brought by compute, algorithms, and large models is obvious. People know the power in their hands is immense—hence today’s conflicts.

If you ask a developer to focus on individuals, their job becomes impossible. Back to food delivery: imagine an algorithm overseeing, with crowd-sourcing included, tens of millions. When building that algorithm, does one truly center a single rider’s experience under algorithmic governance? It’s hard to. That’s the heart of the conflict.

That’s why my pink bubble doesn’t exist. Reality—of class, systems, and interests—has carved us up, and empathy is collapsing. From an algorithm’s abstract vantage point, a person is a flickering dot. In fact that “dot” might be a cluster of 200 people in a zone—it’s not a person, it’s an aggregate. That’s the problem. Which is why I think art related to the internet remains necessary: it brings another dimension to this conflict. Within the system, each stakeholder’s voice has limited penetration.

#### The Retreat of Decentralization

Yang

Another buzzword I hear a lot is “decentralization.” My lay understanding: the central broadcaster used to be the hub, now platforms like Xiaohongshu spawn many micro-opinion leaders. From an artist with a computing background, does this concept matter?

aaajiao

Conceptually, decentralization matters. The term “decentralized” surfaced around the Web 2.0 era. You can even find earlier internet manifestos in the 1980s chanting it. Then came the crypto cycle—before the ChatGPT moment—where “decentralized” became one more storytelling hook, even a keyword for grift. Its meaning kept shifting.

But today, “decentralized” is in decline. AI’s rise, political realities, centralized compute, and Wall Street’s infiltration of crypto—we can call it a mutual embrace—have driven a swing back to centralization. People may not want to say it, but they’re practicing it: returning to the center to chase speed and scale.

Yang

There’s little we can do—perhaps, as artists, we can at least alert people that this is happening.

aaajiao

Exactly. A few years ago I did a project called URLis.LOVE (the domain really was urlis.love). It revisited many earlier works originally written for desktop environments. After a rewrite into JavaScript, you could view them on the web. The piece explored how platforms carve up the internet—especially in Simplified Chinese contexts, where WeChat and Xiaohongshu are two separate worlds. By contrast, the gap between Meta and Google is smaller. Still, platformization by giant firms is a fact. The URL is no longer “love”—it has become a niche behavior. How many people still type a URL into a browser? Even with Google, you search—search is a platform, too. That loss of agency is obvious; you’re folded into an algorithmic process.

In training large models, one site is pivotal: Reddit, which went public last year. A major revenue stream is selling its data to model makers who want real conversational scenes. Google is a buyer.

If you generalize “URL,” then genuine human dialogue online is also independent—yet it’s become model fodder. That’s a harsh reality. Independent, fragile carriers of information get absorbed by today’s algorithms and their real-world demands. It feels, for now, like an inescapable process.

A footnote: when Elon Musk moved to buy Twitter, many thought it a bad deal—judging from Twitter’s revenue. But if you grasp the internet’s deeper logic, Twitter is a trove: years of terse human exchange that exist nowhere else. Reddit is similar but long-form. Both are all valuable.

Another point: once AI algorithms and compute reach a certain level, you can do real-time search—xAI’s Grok is basically there. Query Grok on xAI and it can include live posts and conversations on X into its references. That’s highly efficient and meaningful: humans and AI co-discussing a topic, with AI synthesizing viewpoints and feeding back in real time. That’s valuable—but humans in that loop are pure raw material. To balance that, X has rolled out creator-payout programs—akin to Bilibili’s creator schemes—to encourage original content. You can get paid for your good quality content. It’s a feedback loop, yes, but you’re being consumed. The death of the URL looks like today’s reality: you keep getting pulled in, you keep speaking, even if you think your thought and speech are independent, the system quickly absorbs them.

#### Data-Labeling Labor

Yang

Listening to you, it sounds like a platform’s “support for original content” is really a way to mint its own resource—like mining coal. It needs high-quality content.

aaajiao

Right. You mentioned drawing boxes. Last year I made a work called Minority Algorithm. Part of it looks at gig work in the sharing-economy era. I chose a China-centric lens: a short video where someone says, “I have drawn 5,000 boxes today since I woke up”—the early phase of training AI. There are sweatshops in Africa, too. In Chinese contexts, that work can look “better” than food delivery: it’s repetitive and grinds you down mentally, but at least you’re spared the elements.

In Africa, wages are also low. But that wave has already happened—and the need for that kind of labor is fading. Is there still demand? Yes, but it’s more complex now: think “SQL AI,” for instance. I’ve seen reporting that, for fine-tuning ChatGPT, the human annotators needed higher backgrounds—sometimes a PhD—to handle complex examples. The work ladders up. By now, the simple box-drawing jobs may already be gone. That’s a big problem.

Yang

I’ve looked into box-drawing too—reports say the demand has fallen, because machines can automate the simple parts.

aaajiao

Exactly. The very labor those workers did led to their replacement. It’s like brick-making: once brick making is automated, we don’t need you at the kiln—you’ve taught the machine how to make bricks.

Yang

That’s too fast—the swap happens before you can even know it.

aaajiao

That’s why I included it: there’s a latent crisis here. Humans acquire recognition ability at great cost: two heterosexual parents have a child—or any family form, biologically you start as a fertilized egg—then you receive human education and learn to identify things. That’s years of investment. But the machine can replace that skill entirely after, say, five years—maybe fewer.

Isn’t that a huge crisis for humanity? Once, only people knew: this is a table; that’s a fan; it’s white; it has a circular face; three buttons, press one to start. Isn’t that cognitive process valuable? And yet that value is transferred, cheaply, to a highly centralized model owned by capital—or the state—or some semi-autonomous agent. Shouldn’t we debate this? Again and again? Shouldn’t we probe how benefits shift in this process?

Yang

That kind of reflection—I rarely see it in the Simplified-Chinese internet. Does it exist?

aaajiao

Even “seeing” in Simplified-Chinese spaces is a problem. Of course those reflections exist, but they’re not public debates—because public debate is hard to form. If it did form, many other debates would, too. So I wouldn’t use that as a reference frame.

Take Bill Gates: early on, when forecasting AI and robots, he proposed taxing robots—or taxing AI. In a sense, that’s a feedback mechanism: these systems are plainly replacing humans.

Let’s go a level deeper: why are humanoid robots the focus of the past year or two? In Industry 2.0/2.5, we used robotic arms for assembly. Why pivot to humanoids now? The simple reason: humanoids replace humans themselves. Why not build specialized robots—more automated, more efficient? Because human industrial infrastructure is very hard to rebuild. Humanoids let you skip reinvesting in basic infrastructure.

Swap out the person, roll in the robot. The building stays. You don’t even need to widen doorways. Maybe just bolt a few chargers to the wall, the robots auto-dock when low. That’s a massive cost-saving strategy.

So human replacement will spark debate. In a growth phase, when people in Silicon Valley resolve to remake humanity with tech, they choose to work with government, to wield great power to realize their vision first—and deal with labor substitution, union conflict, and redistribution later, once it’s done.

### 3. Contemporary art is inseparable from power relations

#### Judge art with a “timeline” mindset

Yang

As an artist you have to grasp a very complex system, form ideas, and then express them visually. Isn’t that an impossibly hard job?

aaajiao

“Art” is itself hard to define. What counts as art? It can feel like anything and nothing. So I focus on this: how to make work that responds to the present—technology, lived reality, even real-time politics. How do you offer a view that is yours? And does that view deserve to be seen by more people? Or perhaps your view matters first to you, yet you still sense it could carry value for others—how does that whole apparatus take shape?

It is not as complicated as it sounds. It resembles how we question a large model: much of my work begins as a question I ask myself. What you see is often my process of answering it. As a question and as an answer, the work appears in public settings where it may land—or not. I use this approach to keep things simple and cut away a lot of external noise.

So is the threshold high? I don’t think so. You can ask many questions every day. But we all know people who go a month without one—their only “question” is why their paycheck is missing the ¥200 full-attendance bonus even though they were never late. Our situations differ, our concerns differ, so the questions we ask differ. But is it truly that hard? Not really.

Yang

I think there is a type of artist people dislike: they can talk for two hours, yet the work seems unrelated to the talk. I later realized the core issue: the artist’s real task is to turn thought into the form of art.

We have thinkers and philosophers who build theory. But in the art world, what makes an artist credible is, first, the ability to translate a problem into a visual form; only then “problem consciousness”matters. For example, a philosopher may advance a great idea few can grasp. An artist, by contrast, at least can render that idea into a perceivable work. That, I think, is the artists’ job. Do you agree?

aaajiao

If we speak narrowly about visual art, then of course the first contact with a viewer is visual. But art admits many forms. Many artists now choose the lecture-performance; the visuals may be rough, yet the rhythm of the performance works—and the artwork  works. So there is no fixed hierarchy. It’s a composite outcome. Some artists are weak visually; still, after you attend their lecture-performance about the work, many pieces of art “click.” The work unfolds along a timeline. It does not exist only at a single point in time. Once you visit an artist’s practice at multiple moments; the work grows more interesting. Some works are even prophetic: their force becomes clear only when later events feed back into them.

#### Identity politics

For me, what matters is that a work bears an artist’s own viewpoint. It need not be research-based, but it must answer why it had to be made by you. It remains individualized. Why you and not someone else? Why should I come hear you and see your work?

I look for the thing only “you” can say. That often decides whether I like a artwork—or admire it. When I can read its absolute tie to that person, it stands.

I once did a short interview. While discussing algorithmic governance we also touched on large models—the relation between AI and the artist. Imagine a work circulating online: it might be an image, a video, AR—any medium that travels on the internet.

Then comes the question: Can AI make it? Is it good enough? Not yet. Will it get better? Certainly. I see a clear trend: more kinds of “artworks” will appear—without necessarily having artists. AI will even have “points of view.” So when I judge whether a work merits attention, now and in the near future, I look even more closely at its link to the person who made it.

Yang

So it still comes down to the person—what they care about, and why they handle the problem in that particular way.

aaajiao

Right, and I don't think the point I just made is unique. If you look back, for perhaps ten years or more, identity politics has been visibly reflected in contemporary art, including issues like intersectionality. That is, the value of a person's own lived experience is crucial. More broadly, it is a deep reflection on power relations, on who holds voice, and on discourse itself.

Contemporary art has standards—inescapably. Who sets them? Even education transmits them, shaping how you learn to see. That relies on power relations, and they are far from perfect. Hence the push within identity-politics movements to center the creator’s irreplaceable experience.

This sparks debate: some say certain black artists make “bad” visuals—so what? You cannot substitute their life experience. The work foregrounds the person. Why focus on the person? Because a visual result can be trained into a standard, into a method; at that point, the art can drain away—especially once AI enters the story.

Today you can “paint a Kiki Smith”—early on, Midjourney already had prompts that let you mimic that style. No meaning there.

So we get conflict. A power system tilts toward identity-politics narratives that resist its own structure; conflict follows. Today some say such work has become politically correct and dull. That is because the power system has expanded to absorb identity politics; it has become stylizable, codable. The discourse numbs. Then we must find the next pressure point—rip the fabric of power again. In that tearing, new art and new understandings of art emerge.

Yang

So contemporary art is power relations.

aaajiao

Contemporary art is, inevitably, power relations. Many Simplified-Chinese readings of contemporary art—many artists here among us—remain stuck in traditional Chinese narratives, like the literati-painting mythology. Frankly, it’s absurd. Think of the so-called Song-era masters, such as the scholar-official painters. In today’s social structure you and that imagined figure are not even in the same class. They are at least “red second-generation” heirs doing art; you may be middle class or newly rich—and may not keep the wealth. What are you talking about—“spiritual encounter”? It’s laughable.

#### How we judge contemporary art

Yang

I spent a year in the UK and visited museums and galleries whenever I could. I felt I began to understand contemporary art. But when I went back to home, I was lost again. I’m confused.

Lately I’ve realized why an artist can survive abroad but struggles in mainland China. The mainland runs on a commercial power system; abroad there is another system—museums, independent and research institutions—so artists with low commercial value can still live.

Study it further and you see who backs those institutions: old money, corporate foundations—of course this behavior may still link to commerce. Push further and you can argue Britain still lives off colonial dividends; the chain leads back to empire.

So colonialism is not only about fairness or bullying. Its influence runs much deeper. It shapes the whole Western system. Am I right?

aaajiao

If we must compare China and the “beyond-China” world, I would frame it this way. I won’t describe the whole machine; I follow the main line of conflict: does contemporary art in your society actually affect real politics and social life?

Take a fresh London example. Banksy recently did a new stencil on a wall by a court; it was scrubbed—imperfectly, which almost made it better. During COVID he himself was repeatedly consumed by the market; he even pulled a stunt at auction to destroy his own work.

I think that stunt was weak, but his return to the street matters. When conflict in the real world grows so sharp that an artist like Banksy must go back to the street to make a confrontational piece—and it gets erased—you see that contemporary art in the UK remains a public topic. It still shapes how people interpret the present.

Now look at mainland China. Which artist’s work can realistically pass through layers of censorship and affect the public? Almost none. The “biggest” art story I hear is a society-page one: Fan Zeng’s new marriage and missing funds. That is the difference. In China, contemporary art has virtually no social impact; it speaks to a small circle that already cares. It is a large bubble. In the boom years, the art scene largely abandoned the aim of influencing others through content, so it grew dull and lost drive; when the market sagged, the whole thing sagged.

Yang

As someone who shows in both China and Germany, do you feel different kinds of feedback?

aaajiao

It tracks what I said. Societies treat the content and conversations of contemporary art differently. In China many exhibitions are highly performative—performed for familiar insiders, even for oneself. Many artists there are still in a kind of cultural coloniality, performing for an imagined system outside China—North America or Europe. They believe they have made something of value for that audience. That is the harshest part: some won’t even perform for their local ecosystem—they look down on it: “You don’t read me.” They perform for an imaginary system that does not exist. Meaningless—and brutal.

This mirrors China’s reality. It is an echo chamber, a filter bubble; noise ricochets until it drowns everything. People feel successful; the final proof of past success was sales. Once the market vanishes, the mirage is exposed—yet that mirage still props up artists of all ages in China’s contemporary scene.

Yang

If a work does not sell, some people even lose the ability to keep making contemporary art.

aaajiao

Exactly.

### 4. Prophecy, Negative Space, Dystopia, First Principles, and the Worldview Inherited by LLMs

#### China’s internet as a prophecy for the West

Yang

When we try to think through today’s problems, the West still seems ahead in many respects. Yet in others—say, the mobile internet—we looked to be out in front. The relationship feels tangled. Do you feel that too? For instance, the other day you said China’s internet is a kind of prophecy for the West; I didn’t quite get it at the time.

Even now, the theory we draw on still seems to come mostly from the West. As an artist who moves between China and Europe, do you run into a mismatch on the two sides?

aaajiao

That’s a big question. Back to contemporary art: how many concepts from Chinese contemporary art are actually shared and recognized by everyone? Very few. What do I mean when I say China’s internet is prophetic for Europe? Let me give an example. When I first moved to Berlin, on the U-Bahn or S-Bahn—their public transit—you’d see maybe 70%–80% of people reading books. That was 2017. Yesterday on the S-Bahn, 100% of the people looking at anything were looking at their phones. And what exactly? I always watch—mainly two things: Instagram and TikTok; for messaging it’s WhatsApp.

That’s the prophecy. In 2017, in Shanghai or Beijing that was already an everyday reality, not something to debate. In Europe, it’s only recently become an obvious shift.

Yang

Right—back home there was that meme that on the London Tube everyone reads books and “their public manners are different.” But when  you take London Tube you realize there’s no signal on parts of the Tube; of course people read. If there were internet, most people would be scrolling TikTok too.

aaajiao

That’s precisely the prophetic bit. As for theory: if you look at Chinese contemporary-art scholars and what they cite, it’s basically all theories not made in China. China’s reality becomes an extension field for those theories—it turns into a “phenomenon set.”

Yang

A few years back I got really into Byung-Chul Han. He lives in Berlin, a Korean philosopher.

aaajiao

He’s Korean-German.

Yang

I found his frameworks explain a lot about China. The classics are great too, but they don't seem to be squarely tackling the problems unfolding right now—then I came across him. Is that just how it is?

aaajiao

Reality is messier than our neat talk. In China’s context, if you ask “why?”, you may already have been censored—that’s the point. Why aren’t there such theories? Are there really none? You can keep asking. There are. A small clue: in China, most professors who work on the philosophy of technology sit inside the schools of Marxism—that already tells you a lot.

#### The behavior of the disempowered user

Yang

Technologically we’ve gone toe-to-toe with the U.S., but our theoretical scaffolding hasn’t kept pace. Wired magazine runs issue after issue about how tech reshapes life. Domestic media may cover industries and new tech, but not how it actually changes people’s lives.

What we’re discussing barely shows up in mainstream coverage. There’s a vacuum—and yet on TikTok everything feels so vivid. So traditional media hub  feels like it’s failing one of its jobs: diagnosis and reflection.

I’m thinking of your Minority Algorithm—its subtitle is Internet Void. Could you unpack the “void”?

aaajiao

I actually call it the negative space of the Internet. It’s the “emptiness” we know from Chinese thought—“xing-kong” or the Zen notion of emptiness. But what is that “empty” space here? I do have an answer: it’s the aggregate of human behavior online. When we talk about the internet, we mostly look at the flow of visual media. But our behavior has always been part of the medium. That’s crucial, and it needs to be named. Only when users’ behavior is seen does the internet become a complete object of discussion. Right now we fixate on outputs.

Take xAI or Reddit: we stare at the traces—conversations we left, the likes we tapped—while ignoring the behavior we’re constantly performing. Sure, algorithms can infer where your eyes dwell, but that’s still an output. For media analysis we need to magnify how behavior shapes the internet—and how the internet in turn shapes the way we behave with it.

Once you do that, the politics and the mobilizing potential are obvious. If we admit our present behavior isn’t steering the internet somewhere better—or toward human flourishing—then we should change our behavior. Simple example: can you stop binge-scrolling short videos? That is a behavior. But the system wants maximum deep engagement.

Yang

So behavior means: I scroll shorts; I click links. I’m not seeing why that needs to be “seen.” I don’t get it yet.

aaajiao

Because when you click, most of the time you’re barely conscious of it.

Yang

The act of scrolling feels unworthy of attention.

aaajiao

If you can enlarge your awareness of that act, you might reclaim more choice. Right now you’ve chosen to give up power: years of screen-based interaction have trained you to know what to tap for a little hit of pleasure, what to avoid. Platforms downplay that behavioral layer because they want you to keep engaging by their training. But if you zoom out on your entire online behavior—almost like meditation—or the way you deliberately regulate your breathing on a run, that meta-awareness can change how you use the medium. And the internet could visibly shift.

Yang

It’s hard though. I’ve heard this from so many people: open TikTok, and two hours vanish.

aaajiao

Sure.

Yang

Someone told me some platforms let you turn off the recommender system. Once you do, the feed becomes unbearable—you get bored fast. The algorithm brings so much pleasure. Every day I tell myself to scroll less, and still I can’t resist. Any strategies?

aaajiao

Calling it “impossible” is an excuse. It’s like running: if you want to run faster or more comfortably, you train your core, fix your form—maybe buy better shoes. People have agency. If scrolling TikTok lags, you’ll happily buy a new phone. If you can pay that price, why not make other choices? Yes, it’s hard—but not unachievable.

It takes education and shared norms. Right now we have neither. We use these tools the wrong way; we “participate” in the internet the wrong way. No public consciousness. Think about the new trash sorting policy: we used to toss everything together. Sorting is a pain. In Germany and Japan it’s very strict; once you learn it, it’s no big deal. Why can’t we develop new ways to use the internet—or invent methods that fit individuals better?

Yang

Someone use “blunt” tactics: accountability groups, no phones in bed, timers for minutes per app. I’ve tried them—deleted Xiaohongshu, then reinstalled. It’s painful: can’t leave, don’t want to be trapped.

aaajiao

Exactly. That’s why I made the work. Naming the negative space of the Internet is about nudging people to consider that concept—this possibility—and to crack our habitual frame. That’s the point of art. If it becomes even a small topic, and gradually more people share the concept, then related work can slowly gather into a bigger conversation. Which loops back to why works need an articulated point of view from the artist: without it, you lose the connective tissue and the future possibilities.

#### AI inherits human bias

Yang

What you said made me think about algorithmic spookiness. Change your location and the feed flips. My feeling is in big cities the info feels “higher-quality”; in a remote Chinese village it seems worse. 

aaajiao

It’s very close to traditional supply chains. Take Coca-Cola prices: people assume it’s cheaper in poor places. Often not. Delivery costs raise prices the farther you go.

First, you may not even get the real Coke—that’s one issue. Second, it can be more expensive, with local conditions priced in. Today, algorithms deliver different prices and different “quality” by location, user base, class. In Beijing or Shanghai you buy authentic cosmetics; in Guiyang you may get fakes—because the system assumes you can’t tell, and that’s not a new story. If we pull the thread, it’s an unfair distribution regime—down to infrastructure design. It’s sprawling. Algorithms faithfully inherit those real-world problems—much like how frontier models inherit stereotypes and bias. It’s the same throughline.

Yang

Exactly. In big cities, Freshippo (Hema supermakets) gets you very high-quality food; in small counties the supermarket shelves are full of things I’d never eat. Try to buy something healthy and the cost skyrockets. The internet long promised to “flatten” this. But I’m stuck with poor goods and poor information. That problem’s huge.

aaajiao

Back to Google’s “Don’t be evil.” Back then there was a visible gap between tech and society; the motto stood against that gap. Today the gap has basically closed. Real-world systems and institutions are so entwined now that “don’t be evil” is naïve—irresponsible even. Our discussions of technology have to puncture the bubble that pretends tech is “pure,” and drag it back into real politics and society—because it has already saturated both.

So if a work is about cyberspace and you insist on situating it in pure cyberspace, I find it dull. Many such works are made by younger artists; they haven’t lived enough of the social realities yet. Understandable—but I care less.

I just read a piece about doing contemporary art “from the angle of quantum computing”—lots of visuals and concepts. Good, but I’m not that interested because the viewpoint doesn’t pierce cyberspace. It lacks a reality dimension. To me it’s incomplete.

#### A philosophy of technology that’s been neutered

Yang

I get most information via social platforms. On X (formerly Twitter), the programmers I follow focus on industry. On Xiaohongshu people debate love, freedom, fairness—pure humanities. I found few people try to bridge fields; it’s like each group’s knowledge structure skews what they even notice.

aaajiao

It depends on how far these people get in the industry. Think the “PayPal Mafia”—Peter Thiel, Palantir’s boss, Elon Musk,etc. Running a tech empire and a production machine requires a lot of real-world politics and technology philosophy—and they use those creatively.

For instance, “end-to-end.” Or Palantir’s boss floating—term escapes me—something like a “total art” idea, quite philosophical, used to manage his enterprise.

Take Tesla’s FSD (Full Self-Driving): why only cameras? That’s a philosophical stance. SpaceX too: Musk rebuilt the traditional Silicon Valley playbook with his own worldview. He’s made it work in some ways, but the societal damage in real-world is obvious.

So the people you see obsessed with “one technical point” are mostly operating at the application layer. Hard to get them guided by a worldview. In Chinese-language spheres this is even more so.

Yang

I remember a news story years ago—Google hiring science-fiction writers, because you can engineer tech, but it’s hard to imagine the future.

aaajiao

In the PRC context: new-media art started doing big shows in 2006 at the China Millennium Monument; then with the 2008 Olympics the National Art Museum put on a huge biennial of media/new-media art—maybe still the world’s largest of its kind.

Why could that happen in a country with strict censorship and ideological control? Because the elite of the regime thought tech carried no obvious propaganda or philosophical charge. That’s ignorance—or performative ignorance. They assumed they could segment control over individuals away from “this” part. Same as after 1989: social reform focused only on the economy; political reform fell silent, even regressed.

So in China, this link was cut. In Simplified-Chinese discourse, it’s very hard to find grounded, socialized thinking about technology—let alone philosophy-level thinking. The system needs it to stay a tool layer, an application layer—neutered. That neutering hollows out fundamental innovation and productivity.

Personally, I don’t expect “end-to-end” philosophies—or Palantir-style worldviews—to be articulated first by a Chinese company. What you get instead is Huawei-style militarized management—very PRC—and Tencent and others touting “wolf culture.” How much do those contribute to technology and the future? Not much. For shipping applications and getting new things out the door? Absolutely.

Yang

Nowadays AI talk is all U.S. vs China—we look advanced. I saw a piece that years ago the state poured money into infrastructure—like the “East Data, West Computing project” that sort of thing—so the regime “picked the right track.” But were the motives mixed, and we stumbled into this lane by accident?

aaajiao

Deng said “science and technology are the primary productive forces”—that slogan goes way back. It’s a core priority—also dictated by modern Chinese history: “lagging behind means being beaten,” and Mao’s “political power grows out of the barrel of a gun”—you need to make weapons. So tech’s role in that system has always been clear. But technology must “follow the Party”. That’s why it’s crippled.

If we use a game metaphor: the “tech tree” has always been mis-specced. One branch never gets any skill points; the other side tracks the latest rhythms and trends. That’s the reality I see in China, and it profoundly shapes society. In PRC-context media art, many artists don’t realize they’re repeating language that’s essentially shaped by the Party.

#### Disruptive innovation

Yang

When you mentioned FSD, you also said “disruptive.” What did you mean?

aaajiao

The backdrop is Silicon Valley’s doctrine of disruptive innovation—first breaking prior technical paths; now, in my view, extending to breaking social institutions and existing social contracts. Look at the Biden administration’s posture toward Big Tech: antitrust and break-ups; lots of regulation; concern that, say, crypto or platform monopolies will crush smaller innovators—so regulate.

The Valley titans say no—we need disruption now, not regulation. Gather resources and build first. In Chinese we’d call it: “pool resources to accomplish big things.” It’s oddly like a one-party slogan—and it’s happening. Once you do that, you’re breaking the existing order.

Yang

So understanding “disruptive innovation” seems foundational to your theory.

aaajiao

It’s simply what’s happening now. The Valley even minted new jargon—some call it a fresh “accelerationism.” They’ve built a new narrative. Say Meta poaching AI scientists with three-year packages worth tens of millions of dollars. They need a story, and not a boutique one: a narrative the whole Valley can converge on so people will join.

Yang

Is disruptive innovation good or bad?

aaajiao

It’s not that binary. For example, has end-to-end created new possibilities? You can see the results. But the figure most identified with end-to-end also waded directly into U.S. politics—clearly influencing it. Same person, both things.

Yang

Could you explain “end-to-end”?

aaajiao

Take self-driving. Google’s approach puts lots of specialized sensors on the car; scan the environment; multiple devices work together; algorithms fuse the data; and you rely heavily on high-precision maps.

Tesla’s tech tree is specced differently. “End-to-end” there means: humans drive by vision; why can’t the machine do the same? You go from input all the way to control—end to end. So it uses only on-board cameras—no lidar, no other sensing hardware. Everything rests on vision-based large-model algorithms. Before the ChatGPT moment they still used traditional components; with frontier models, the model became the centerpiece.

Yang

Does that tie back to first principles?

aaajiao

Yes—first principles in action.

#### Crypto seen through a dystopian lens

Yang

By “dystopia,” do you mean: “I just don’t buy the utopian line”? Utopia is that ideal world; tech sells lots of utopian use cases. The internet promised a flat, egalitarian world. Dystopia is: I didn’t believe the flattening in the first place?

aaajiao

Exactly. Take Bitcoin. I encountered it early—around 2010 I made a piece of art about it. If I’d mined then, you might not see me now—one coin was around $7 then; today it’s $120,000. I even phoned a friend: “Should we mine? Feels new.” He said, “We live in Shanghai—those rigs run hot.” I said, “Right, hassle—let’s skip it.”

Instead I made art. The show was Placebo. Studying Bitcoin’s machinery, I found a strong utopian impulse—ditch the central bank. Back then I understood compute as CPU power; you could even mine with a CPU. It felt ideal.

But I then noticed: use different machines and your mining efficiency changes. So it was never the perfect world it claimed. It couldn’t be detached from reality. I ended up making Poor Mining I. Intel gave me a machine; I turned it into a web-based miner—open a page and it actually mined. I miked the machine: once the page opened the fans screamed and the box vibrated. Amplified, it sounded like a traditional oilfield or mine. Loud. I used that to link virtual and physical.

I never believed Bitcoin could exist outside reality as a pure cyberspace utopia. From the start I assumed it was tightly bound to the world. Years later, Wall Street and crypto culture fully embraced each other; the U.S. even moved toward state-backed stablecoin systems. It’s all proof you can’t conjure a utopia beyond human society—whether in cyberspace or meatspace. Same logic applies going forward.

If one day we colonize Mars and declare it a utopia—you’re being conned. That’s my long-held view. Because humans are flawed, our societies can’t be perfect; we never complete the circle. We only glimpse the cracks.

As an artist, my job is to keep pointing at those incomplete, broken edges—at the grayness in human nature and, sometimes, the rare gleams of grace. When do those moments surface? When should we attend to them? What do they teach us? That’s why my work exists.

#### AI emergence

Yang

AI runs on machine learning; machine learning runs on neural nets; neural nets copy the brain’s architecture—layers of neurons. I’ve heard the human brain has about 120 billion neurons, and from birth our senses take in data 24/7. Some say: if AI gets complex enough—enough layers, enough units, enough data—it’ll have feelings, like human being. It’s already a black box; even engineers can’t predict what a big model will output. So the more we study AI, the more it seems brain-like.

Which leads to: perhaps the ultimate form of life will be silicon. Humans are just the loading program. People are costly—food, waste, emotions. Silicon will inevitably displace carbon. Your take?

aaajiao

I recently read a recollection by a former OpenAI employee about how “emergence” happened. Back when Sam Altman and Elon Musk clashed—and Elon pulled out—the technical path wasn’t obvious. But OpenAI did one right thing: they bet on breadth. Why “large” models? Because size matters—bigger got smarter, not narrower. As someone mildly technical, I still find that uncanny.

Flip it around: how do we recognize a learned person? By all the different things they've seen and experienced. Our life experience as we age—breadth that’s hard to replicate. “Read ten thousand books, travel ten thousand miles,” as the proverb goes—messy inputs, not tidy.

OpenAI kept scaling breadth; once it got broad enough, efficiency leapt; the results suddenly “emerged.” Could humans build something that exceeds our own basic structure? I’m skeptical. But if you aggregate humans into a single “individual,” that entity should surpass any one of us. Humanity’s collaborative artifacts—our laptops, our weapons—already do. No one person could make them alone.

So if a frontier model aggregates the civilization-wide corpus, it should surpass any single human. But it’s still a human collective that built it. Our problem is that we can’t merge our minds into one.

Even so, I still worry. The wave of “new fascism” talk—I was just at a global fascism panel in Berlin—comes from this tech tree. A single giant model is a very literal avatar of fascism who can unify voice, using a single chat interface that channels vast collective knowledge. That’s scary.

#### Beware monocultures

Yang

If AI grows powerful enough, how do we prevent harm? There’s no obvious answer.

aaajiao

The EU was the first region with a clear legal framework for AI—that’s where Europeans excel: humanistic reasoning. Also, they lack tech giants with the heft to override politics, so laws bite. But it creates friction. If Google wants to roll out a new feature in Europe, it needs to pass EU review first—so you see delays, and some features get modified.

So yes: people see the risks. The tension is whether to move fast to unleash productivity, or regulate to keep it orderly. China is a third pole: put AI through an outright censorship stack—cripple a general-purpose AI and make it “follow the Party.” That’s a different world entirely.

Yang

What would you choose? Disruptive innovation needs rough growth, but Europe’s humanistic stance reins things in. People say that’s why Europe lags the U.S. and China in internet tech: regulate early, no wild growth; productivity lags. Isn’t that a real problem?

aaajiao

Which is why parts of Silicon Valley have “gone dark,” minting “new accelerationism” to make that narrative stick.

I’ve lived in Europe for years. In Berlin, sure—some tech isn’t widely deployed; life is less convenient. But do I need all of it? Back to our train example: must we read on the subway? Must we stare at phones? We can also do nothing. There’s no single correct behavior; pluralism matters.

What frightens me is everyone only looking at phones underground. The world should be multipolar: a hyper-competitive China doing 996 and building Party-aligned models; a regulated Europe that preserves the right to read on trains; and a Silicon Valley of disruptive innovation that’s willing to lash itself to state power to “make it happen.” That triptych already exists.

So are we fully homogenized? Not yet. But I’m wary because I can see two poles amassing power and influence. That’s what any polity fears: monoculture, a single standard. When that system errs, correction becomes ruinously expensive—maybe impossible.

#### An AI race in Cold War colors

Yang

Competition seems inevitable when national interests are on the line—quantum computing, say. If one side gets a breakpoint, it can crack encryption. That triggers the “cram school” panic we know in China: don’t cram and you’ll be left delivering takeout and being exploited. The AI narrative feels stuck in competition language.

aaajiao

That’s why the macro trend is “an AI race”—and why chips and supply chains get used to throttle China. I saw a Goldman Sachs chart with absolute numbers for global compute: roughly the U.S. at over 60%, China at ~20%, Japan ~10%, the EU ~6%; only those four regions have meaningful capacity, the rest negligible. That frame makes it easy to think Cold War.

Why “compete” and not “collaborate”? Cold War thinking. People are making national-security arguments: AI is national security; compute is a strategic resource. The whole story is situated in that frame, not in a truly collaborative, species-level one. It’s regional Cold War logic that everyone’s now adopted.

Then dig into why we’re in Cold War mode. About China’s “involution”—it’s not just “hyper-competition.” It’s when the actions of competition no longer produce value. You pile on degrees thinking it will help; you still can’t get a job. You do a PhD you shouldn’t need; you consume more public resources and your own years for little return. That’s involution. And the backdrop is a distribution problem.

Globally we already produce enough protein to feed everyone. Energy and goods are over-supplied. The sticking point is distribution. In China it’s even starker. Without changing the distribution regime, people can only “grind”—and still get nowhere. Then comes “lying flat,” a defensive coping strategy.

In that Cold War backdrop—and the PRC’s present reality—this whole AI race narrative is abnormal: an emergency mindset. But it was born in conflict and rides conflict. It spreads fast. If you treat humanity as a whole, shared interests aren’t respected here. This is not a conversation about the common good.

Yang

Hearing you, I realize: if we actually collaborated on LLMs, many problems could be solved quickly. But that’s utopian. We have unprecedented productive forces—and still we channel them into zero-sum races.

aaajiao

True. And yet we have had nearly a century—since World War II—of relatively fewer wars and famines, globally. Humanity can do that. But cycles recur. A new plague—we had COVID. New tech trees—AI lit up. New wars—they’re here. Like business cycles, it’s hard to escape.

We’re in a new turn now. As an artist, I still feel a duty—to keep hope for myself, and tuck hope into the work. My “dystopia” isn’t hope-less; it’s a way to talk about the good by starting from what’s real.

### 5. Migration and Identity

#### Sinophone

Yang

I do agree with this: you only see your own country clearly once you’ve left it. The other day I scrolled past an interview with Zha Jianying in which she said: I suggest everyone “quit China” for a year—don’t speak Chinese, don’t eat Chinese food, etc. Do that and a lot of things will feel different. I think your turn to “identity” is partly related to your move to Berlin. When I first went abroad I felt all kinds of difference—that “culture shock”—and it pushed me to think about many other questions.

I’ve also noticed, in recent years, clusters of Chinese-language intellectuals in places like Europe and Japan: they express opinions in Chinese from there, and I’ve started following what they make and think. How do you look at identity now? Yours is more layered—you have an online identity and an offline one, you’re a minority abroad, and there was that period of being stuck in Berlin during COVID. How do you see your practical circumstances in Germany? How well have you “integrated” into this society?

aaajiao

I watched that conversation between Zha Jianying and Yuan Li too—very interesting. I used to watch her on Behind the Headlines with Wen Tao, and later she published with Oxford—lots of discussion of China. I’m actually glad she finally “came out” politically in public. She used to be ambiguous—about the regime, about how to read China—but this time she crossed her own threshold.

I can understand why: she had a lot of privilege in China. It isn’t just family—say, her brother, her wider kin—but as part of an elite generation, she had resources and privilege. That privilege becomes a heavy burden. So sometimes what she says now is almost funny: only now you’ve figured this out?

Take one example: she was already a “returnee” in the 1980s—what was then called overseas Chinese. That term is entirely defined and managed by the Chinese Communist Party. She’s carried that label all along; in a sense she carried a piece of the Party—not as a participant, but as someone assigned a common mission.

Overseas Chinese is a fascinating—and, frankly, frightening—united-front term. Look at Malaysia or other Southeast Asian societies: people who are second or third generation are still called overseas Chinese—that’s alarming.

After COVID, many younger people who left China realised: I’m definitely not “overseas Chinese.” A lot of them began to describe themselves as diaspora Chinese. That gets complicated, and the community is larger. In the U.S., for instance, how do Chinese groups define themselves? American Chinese? Asian American? Many also claim diaspora, especially first-generation or 1.5-generation immigrants. There’s an identity bind there.

Then there’s the more current idea of the Sinophone—more grounded in place. It’s contentious; there are different strands. Some Sinophone studies even bracket China out, precisely to avoid being swallowed by that “overseas Chinese” logic.

Because much of the scholarship begins in film and literature—and because of audiences—this identity is still evolving. But one thing I can say—thinking again of that Zha Jianying interview, which at times I found quite ironic—is that when I moved to Berlin, I quickly realised the first step is to shed “overseas Chinese.”

That’s crucial—and the hardest. Many people I know still live as “overseas Chinese.” Can you then become diaspora Chinese? That depends a lot on local community. If the community is weak, it’s hard to sustain that identity, and people default to the process of what you called “integration.” I don’t love that word. Integration is two-way. In the old days, the U.S. could still claim a “welcome culture.” As things polarised, that faded. Integration to what? North America still does better than Europe. In Germany, the AfD (a right-wing populist party) has risen by tapping Germans’ own identity bind: for a long time you couldn’t simply say “I enjoy being German.” History—World War II, the Holocaust—made that kind of pride suspect. “Germanness” wasn’t encouraged as a public identity. But for young people here, and for, say, German citizens of Turkish descent, how should identity be understood?

If, after all these years of identity politics, people in one place still cannot say “I am German,” what do you fuel? The right. The right tells you: say it—be proud to be German.

Then comes the question of who gets to define “German.” That’s delicate—and it’s a powerful political weapon. The reality you meet in Germany is this: living in Berlin, have you ever been invited to become German? Never. Living in the EU, have you been invited to become “European”? Also never. So who are you? Only you can decide. If you want to speak in a Sinophone register, you need deep ties with the local community.

As a first-generation immigrant, I find it hard to reach the Sinophone condition I actually admire—retaining what your identity carries culturally, while also engaging deeply with local cultures. It still has to be two-way. In North America—because of people and culture—it may be easier; in Europe it’s very hard. Here many end up as diaspora Chinese because you’re neither-nor. You’ve stepped away from “overseas Chinese,” no longer state-defined. But Sinophone needs a thick enough community; without that bridge for new arrivals or even the second generation, your identity stays in flux. Layer on local identity conflicts and it gets very hard to define—certainly not by asking whether you’ve “integrated.” Even if your German is native-like, in the German social logic you’re still not German when you walk down the street.

Yang

You just mentioned overseas Chinese and diaspora Chinese…

aaajiao

Overseas Chinese means Chinese abroad; diaspora Chinese means Chinese in dispersion. And then comes Sinophone. That is you might be second- or third-generation and still run into identity dilemmas: how do you understand an identity shaped by family—or by your face? Does that identity really belong to you?

#### Refugees

Yang

There was a time when “world citizen” sounded utopian—but it doesn’t exist. The U.K. is exclusionary too. Germany seems different. I heard—can’t confirm—that Germany took in 4 million Syrians. That’s from a Dutch friend. The rough idea was that Germany was very tolerant of refugees and took them in, which then caused social problems and a backlash. I couldn’t understand why Germany did it. He said it was guilt over World War II. He kept it simple and didn’t really explain.

It feels like Germany is tolerant at its core, and then only after some serious incidents did exclusion rise. So it’s not like the U.K. It seems unique in Europe—a place where large numbers of refugees can start new lives.

aaajiao

Syrian refugees did arrive in large numbers, and after Russia invaded Ukraine, Germany also took in war refugees—about two million, across Europe Germany accepted the most. Within the EU, it really did the best job on war-refugee reception. But you can’t analyse this with simple utopia or simple morality. First, Germany is a very confident polity and civilisation. Why shouldn’t I help the vulnerable? If I can, I should. That is a virtue. Second, look at social reality: Germany has long had a low birth rate. Since the postwar era it has brought in various groups to rebuild the country: think of Turkish workers, now in their second or third generation. Move here and you’ll see there were also Vietnamese—“Gastarbeiter,” guest workers. You’re invited to build the country, but your living conditions are tightly controlled.

After that period, German society tried to make amends. Vietnamese contract workers in the GDR, for instance, faced inhumane rules—women were barred from pregnancy; housing was uniform; social and private life strictly controlled. They were invited because they were cheap labour. Postwar, many Eastern Europeans also came to mines; even Italians came as workers. This is the history. As Germany recovered—it entered a golden age in the 1950s—a strong consensus formed: help the vulnerable. So when the wars began, it was generous to Middle Eastern refugees, and again to Ukrainians. That is its civilisational stance.

But can a confident civilisation avoid mistakes? Of course not. In hindsight, the state overestimated society’s capacity, creating new social tensions. For example: Germany has a lot of social housing to help low-income residents. When refugees arrived, some of those units were reallocated to people deemed more vulnerable. Did lower-middle Germans resent it? Deeply. If, at that moment, you compare suffering, refugees clearly had it worse. But once the other person has a flat and you do not, you feel worse off—yet society doesn’t fully recognise your situation. That breeds friction, and political forces channel it into votes and into new blocs—reshaping the landscape. It’s complicated.

And since labour shortages go back to the postwar period, does bringing in people make sense? How do you resolve the shortage? The hope was to turn new arrivals into “qualified labour” through Germany’s systems—first as labour, a new-era Gastarbeiter. If they engage deeply, perhaps the state gradually grants clearer citizenship and fuller participation. That’s what Germany imagines. But it’s also somewhat utopian and overconfident. A community carrying another civilisation—and war trauma—won’t be remoulded so easily. There are many conflicts here.

From a macro view, I agree with the EU’s ethos of helping the vulnerable—even, at times, at great cost. That’s the bright side of humanity. But humans are pragmatic; details bite; real problems pile up. Has the friction torn at the EU? I think yes.

Yang

So labour shortages were part of the calculation.

aaajiao

Of course. Don’t romanticise power. Power is made of many complex parts, including interests. In the Chinese context, the discourse on state power has always been deeply lacking. This is because China is not, by and large, a society ruled by law; it is a superficial social order built on a moral code. Moral perfection is therefore part of the regime’s legitimacy, but this has become both utopian and unrealistic.

A Chinese politician can’t admit fault: to admit moral fault is to vanish, and with it legitimacy. In Europe, governments admit fault and adjust. But society pays for those adjustments. When a government admits error, the costs borne by society can be huge.

Yang

It feels like Europe is always experimenting.

aaajiao

China, too—every regime is trial-and-error in its own way. Is electoral democracy the final human solution? Definitely not. Is a more authoritarian system necessarily evil? In some moments, maybe not—that’s a harsh reality. But across a longer arc, it can be disastrous for the people living under it. So it’s hard to absolutise any system as pure right or pure wrong. When we look dynamically, most people will arrive at their own choices.

### 6. Metadata as a Creative Method

#### Visual metadata

Yang

I’ve got a few technical questions. Your tools feel very cutting-edge. A decade ago you were already using algorithms in Karesansui; that tattoo project used 3D scanning; later I saw you experiment with something like magnetic levitation and even VR games. Do you hold specific aesthetic convictions—like which medium to choose, how to handle the tech, what standard of beauty to aim for? Any leanings?

aaajiao

Many of my works are made with my studio. If there’s a technical part we can’t nail ourselves, we bring in specialists. Even then our approach tends to be simple. I try to use technology deftly—we rarely brute-force a tool. If something won’t yield, we switch methods. The reason is simple: the internet is overwhelmingly rich, and it’s fundamentally a visual medium, so there are near-endless ways to work with it. Within the internet’s architecture, the visual is, in fact, simple.

Of course, I have my own preferences—shaped gradually by making, and shaped alongside the internet’s own evolution. For instance, take iOS 26: its interface marks a new era with a heavy use of transparent, glass-like effects. That “glass” is more than UI decoration.

It reframes how we understand spatial computing.  It isn’t merely visual; it’s a structured visual, a functional visual.

That’s why I often say I focus on metadata. Brought back to the image: does visual form have an equivalent of metadata? Yes. Find it, excavate it, and make it part of the work. In Minority Algorithm, for example, I use  TikTok app to edit all the interstitial transitions that exploded in the short-video scene. I used it on purpose, because those motion presets are today’s most ubiquitous metadata. Tacky or boring to some, yes—but they saturate our visual field. I deliberately combine them with short clips you’d never encounter in a casual scroll to produce friction.

So it’s of the moment, yet also an intentional contemporary-art choice. That’s been my way of working with images: I mine a kind of visual metadata and build more complex visual relations out of it. It’s a methodological approach—a visual algorithm of my own that keeps absorbing new material. Run through this algorithm, you get a visual outcome at the level of a finished work, fully in service of the work’s viewpoint. There’s a clear lineage because there’s a clear supporting system behind it.

Yang

Your pieces are delightfully concise, especially when some artists overload their concepts. For instance, your work Prompt / Engineering features an AI-generated, manga-style figure who literally voices the prompt which generates her. It’s so concise. That kind of transmutation is crucial for making art, but hard to explain in words.


aaajiao

Right—and that’s often each artist’s private method. Some people are born with it, some are trained. If anything, I’m sensitive. I wouldn’t claim great talent here, but I am attuned. I encountered online visuals very early. In high school I was making personal websites in hand-written HTML, before most JavaScript was in the picture. I’ve watched the internet’s information logic evolve into where visuals are now.

That gives me an advantage. I can attend to metadata and apply that way of thinking to image-making because I started early and never stopped. Today, say someone makes a simple “prompt” piece—three elements: an AI-generated image, AI-generated facial animation, and a synthetic voice reading the prompt. That’s consistent with how I’ve long built works. If another artist adopts a similar recipe without a lineage, it can feel simplistic.

When I do it, it reads as grounded because I’ve been working this way for 10 or 20 years. And that piece was a quick response to the ChatGPT moment, so it stands up: it has meaning on a timeline. If I made it this year, unless no one had touched the idea, it would feel weaker. Doing it then showed I already understood the “tech tree” structurally, and that takes labour and need a sharp response. Some media-art works matter because of their position in time. I also have a strong need: I want them to stay effective longer. I don’t want a work to end as a prototype from that one moment. Five years later it should still read as diagnosing not just a “new tech,” but how those elements have reshaped lived reality. That demand guides the conception—and what you see is a complete result.

Yang

It feels like metadata is a basic mode of thinking for you. Can you go deeper?

aaajiao

Put simply: most of us think visually now. If you strip an image down and can’t reduce it further, what remains is metadata. Take the tool we’re on—Microsoft Teams. What do I see as metadata? First, a frame: a window. Inside it, real-time video, with sound. More granular: buttons on the window for human interaction. For me, that’s the metadata, done.

Yang

That’s a vital way to think. It reminds me of something a teacher once said: don’t start by describing the picture—describe the space it’s in. Is it on a wall? What kind of frame? Set all that first; then talk about the image. People often leap into abstraction too fast. 

aaajiao

This is a process of building technology, and of course, it’s at the application level. When it gets back to an application level and needs a visual GUI (Graphical User Interface), why does it need a window? Why is it such an important piece of metadata?

I can give an example. When we were talking about iOS 26 earlier, this transparent glass is what’s breaking the concept of the window. In Apple's entire interaction logic going forward, you will see that many windows will disappear. It uses a kind of interaction based on that glass moment; it's a communication that has momentum. The window isn’t important. The window is reality. Reality and the entire field of vision you see is the window that has already been blurred out. The interaction inside is the transparent glass.

So in its user interface design, this represents a step forward in how its entire product line, and its entire company, understands the future of how humans will read and interact with information. We can't call it progress; it's a huge experiment.

So this kind of understanding and analysis can influence the visual choices of future works. Because a company this influential, or rather, a worldview like this, will affect the way humans observe things. This method will affect how people understand a visual presence. So I pay attention to these things. I pay attention to why it does this. What is it trying to do behind it all? And behind this user interface, there is a strong link to computing power, to spatial computing, and it must adapt to the possibilities of the AI era. It is a very scientific method for how humans can understand information, one guided by validation-driven design.

So I feel that as an artist in this field, I must pay attention to this matter. What's more, it has done the user education for me. This is not something an artist’s vision can teach on its own. For example, I could invent a user interface and say, “What's the meaning of this?”... Yes, you can do that, you can go against the grain. But I'm more inclined to go with the flow, to find that part of the circle I mentioned that isn’t quite round. That conflict, or that kind of friction, is more compelling. It’s more compelling than if Apple makes a transparent glass, and I go and make something different and say, “This is the future I imagine.” That doesn't have much meaning to me. Because a meaningful future is based on consensus, and a work of art cannot create a consensus. What a work of art can do is explore the friction between a perfect imagination and reality, and the conflict that comes from human imperfection. You can dig very deep into those parts with a work of art. But when you simply want to create another set of standards, as an individual artist, I think that work isn't very meaningful.

#### Identity, Empowerment and Resistance in the Digital Age

Yang

The thinking here feels very first-principles to me.

aaajiao

“First principles” is very Silicon-Valley as a narrative. Is it new? Not really. What’s new is getting everyone to believe it—and to live it. People across layers get ideologically aligned; it becomes a profitable worldview. Silicon Valley achieved that, right?

Yang

Right. Maybe when something like this is launched with a major product from a very important company, everyone just accepts it all at once. But in reality, it's a philosophy from a much earlier time, one that was explored from the most fundamental level.

Looking at your website, I feel your creative path is very coherent, and it seems your output has increased in recent years. So, does your creative process happen in phases? Are there times when you get stuck? Do you have different creative stages?

aaajiao

Definitely. In recent years, people—and their behaviour—have taken up more weight in my work, while the tech itself has receded. Even before the AI surge, Silicon Valley and China’s internet were mostly busy slicing applications finer and finer. There were few revolutions. That gave me time to ask where the work should go; I started focusing on human behaviours. A big turning point was User, Love, High-frequency Trading (2017), when identity entered the frame. From there, I wasn’t just looking at society through tech; I was also looking from the human side, and from an abstract social vantage. Multiple dimensions. Once those dimensions connected, it opened up all sorts of combinations—more layers to build into the work.

After AI exploded, the topics multiplied—plus there are visible shifts in real-world politics, like the global rightward turn. Add my own move from China to Europe—the identity shift—and there’s a lot to do.

Yang

You had a phase on “users,” later “players.” “User” makes me think about our public identity: you get the most attention as a user. Every website treats each behaviour, each scroll as a data point. It’s odd: on your birthday, it’s the commercial sites and banks that send greetings and coupons—that’s where I feel “seen.” “Player,” though, is fuzzier for me.

aaajiao

“User” varies by where you live. After time in Europe, I saw the “user” I worked with was mainly anchored in China. In an atomised society, your reassigned identity is “user.” With algorithmic governance and app-layer governance—and with the state embedded in those apps—you’re moulded by a single tool-stack into a new user.

In Europe people also live on their phones, so they recognise being users—but they also hold other identities: union member, professional-association member. Those are missing in China’s public sphere. You end up as a disenfranchised user, because you’re also a disenfranchised citizen—barely a “citizen” at all. That linkage is strong. 

Looking back at the exhibition User, Love, High-frequency Trading  now, eight years on, it still stands—because I understood China’s internet ecology and the social reality it shapes. People today are even more users than before. Living in Europe now, when I present the work there, I preface it heavily—and turn those conditions into a kind of warning: when a regime goes extreme, you may see a Chinese-style user predicament. Europe should avoid that. Some things are prophetic because they can get worse; the work then reads as a prophecy.

“Player” is about how, under a society with little formal power, or under similar internet relations—you should empower yourself. That buzzword “empower” runs through contemporary art and far beyond. The core difference is agency. Players act. They build within rules, even find bugs and exploit them for meaning. That is the classic sense of “player.” So that “player” line was, in part, encouragement: don’t worry about being a user. We can be users and players. Players can rip the rules a little—and it can be simple if you just try. But you need an identity shift: recognise “I am a user,” but I want to become a player.

#### Silicone Blade

Yang

“Player” sounds like a stance. Your work feels like a steady, oblique resistance. You’re a player within the system. What’s your strategy? Step forward and risk, like Zha Jianying did, and face the fallout? Keep that subtle resistance and balance? 

aaajiao

We rarely get to define whether a work is “subtle.” Power decides when it wants you silent. Power is everywhere, not just in the China we know well—Europe too, with many political forces and agendas. Why are literature and art always censored, canceled, or pressured ? Because creative work is resilient; it outlives a slogan. That’s the problem.

And when a work reaches that point—when it’s resilient—you carry more responsibility. I don’t want a piece to be propaganda for a single political spectrum. It should stay open. I dislike the easy move: visually insulting something. That’s easy. It dodges the real questions. Whether someone is far right, far left, or wavering, they are still human. Should they be erased for their politics? No. The work must not be the knife. It can pose as a knife, but the blade is silicone. The hard part is keeping the posture of a blade while being made of silicone.

Yang

That’s my last question. Anything to add?

aaajiao

Just this—about artists as a group. As of our recording today—18 September 2025—we live in a time of rapid change. Whether you’re established or just begin to use art to express yourself, the pressure is immense: from society and politics, from making a living, from the internet tearing attention apart. It’s a tangle. 

Perhaps this conversation can be a record of one thing: that at least for now we still feel joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure. We can still smell, feel hot and cold. Our tongues still flinch at bitterness. All the human ways of probing the world are still intact. As long as we don’t lose these, art—and creative work more broadly—won’t vanish. I want that on record. If we revisit this in ten years, I hope we still have that capacity.
